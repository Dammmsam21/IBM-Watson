# 11 — Core Concepts & Domain Knowledge

The tools only make sense if you understand the *business* they serve. This section covers the concepts the JD assumes you know (or will learn fast).

---

## 1. Secondary research (a.k.a. desk research)
Using **already-published / existing data** — news, databases, filings, reports — rather than collecting new data yourself (that's *primary* research, e.g., surveys/interviews). This entire role is secondary research: searching databases and synthesizing findings into insight.

**Good secondary research =** right sources → efficient searching → **cross-checking across sources** → synthesis → a clear "so what." Always note your source and date.

---

## 2. Competitive / market intelligence
Gathering and analyzing information about **markets, industries, competitors, and prospective clients** to inform strategy and business development. In this role you produce competitive intelligence so partners can target and approach the right companies effectively.

---

## 3. Business development (BD) in a professional-services firm
How firms like Deloitte **win work**: identify targets → research them → build relationships with decision-makers → demonstrate relevant expertise → pursue and win the engagement. Unlike product sales, it's **relationship- and credibility-led** and often long-cycle. Your research fuels every step.

Key BD terms:
- **Target / prospect** — a company the firm wants as a client.
- **Pursuit** — an active, organized effort to win a specific opportunity.
- **Pursuit materials / collateral** — the documents (decks, profiles, proposals) used to pursue.
- **Pipeline** — the set of opportunities being worked.
- **Trigger event** — a change (new CFO, M&A, IPO, restatement, auditor rotation) that creates a reason/opportunity to engage.

---

## 4. Audit & Assurance (A&A) basics — your business unit
- **Audit:** independent examination of a company's financial statements to give an opinion on whether they're fairly stated. Public companies are required to be audited.
- **Assurance:** broader services that increase confidence in information (e.g., ESG/sustainability assurance, controls reports).
- **The "Big Four":** Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG — the main competitors in this space.
- **Auditor rotation / tenure:** companies periodically change or re-tender their auditor — a prime **trigger event** for BD.
- **Independence:** an auditor must be independent of the client, which shapes which services a firm can sell to an audit client.

> **Why this matters for targeting:** companies that are growing, going public, doing acquisitions, changing CFOs, or reaching auditor-rotation points are the most promising audit targets — and the databases help you spot exactly those signals.

---

## 5. Company structure (the JD asks for this)
- **Corporate family tree:** parent company → subsidiaries → branches. Knowing the structure tells you *who actually buys the audit* and how a "company" is really organized (D&B Hoovers/OneSource show this well).
- **Public vs. private:** public companies file with regulators (rich data in Capital IQ/LSEG/Bloomberg); private companies need PitchBook/Capital IQ/D&B for insight.
- **Governance:** **board of directors**, **audit committee**, and **C-suite** (CEO, **CFO**, controller). For audit BD, the **CFO and audit-committee chair** are usually the key decision-makers (research them in BoardEx).

---

## 6. Storyboarding (named explicitly in the JD)
Planning the **narrative** of a deck/report before building it. A simple model:
**Situation → Complication/Insight → "So what" → Recommendation/Action.**
Each slide carries one message (in its title), and the messages read in sequence as a logical argument. This is the skill that turns raw database output into something an executive will act on.

---

## 7. Putting it together — the research-to-deliverable flow
1. **Define the question** (what does the partner need, and why?).
2. **Plan sources** (which of the nine tools answers which part?).
3. **Gather** firmographics, financials, news, people/relationships, industry context.
4. **Validate** by cross-checking sources; note dates and provenance.
5. **Analyze** in Excel; find the trend/insight.
6. **Storyboard**, then build the deck (PowerPoint) + report/summary (Word).
7. **Quality-check** for accuracy, clarity, and "so what."
8. **Deliver** to US stakeholders with an executive summary.
